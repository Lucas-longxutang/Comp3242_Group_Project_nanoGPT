Architectural Ablation & Quantization in Small Transformers

Madhav, Ricky, Tom, Yunkai

Section 1

The Question

Modern Transformers rely on design choices made for large datasets.

What actually survives when we shrink everything down to a small model and a small dataset?

Section 2

Experimental Lens

  • • Model trained: NanoGPT (~10M parameters).
  • • Dataset: Tiny Shakespeare.
  • • Fully controlled ablation modifying only one parameter at a time. Optimizer, dataset, and capacity remain fixed.

Section 3

The Design Space

We structured the study across 7 distinct axes to evaluate how architectural components scale down.

Systematic Variations Tested:

  • Positional Encodings: Learned vs. RoPE vs. ALiBi vs. None
  • Normalization: Pre-LN vs. Post-LN vs. RMSNorm
  • Attention Heads: 1 vs. 8 vs. 12 Heads
  • Activation Functions: GELU vs. SwiGLU vs. ReLU
  • Residual Connections: Standard vs. Scaled vs. None
  • Context Length: 128 vs. 256 vs. 512 Tokens
  • Weight Precision: Standard FP32 vs. Ternary {-1, 0, 1}

Section 4

Overall Results

  • • Standard baseline model establishes a validation loss of 1.69.
  • • Baseline training completes in approximately 730 seconds.
  • • Architectural changes triggered massive performance variance across runs.
  • • Worst-performing configuration spiked validation loss to 3.35.
  • • Best-performing configuration reduced validation loss down to 1.47.

Section 5

Residual Connections

  • • Acts as a critical skip pathway that bypasses transformer blocks.
  • • Preserves the vital flow of gradients during optimization.
  • • Removing pathways causes training to completely fail.
  • • Validation loss spikes sharply to 3.35, collapsing output to noise.
  • • Proves that skip connections are not optional even at micro-scale.
  • • Scaled Residuals perform nearly identically to standard with a 1.68 loss.

Section 6

Context Length

  • • Standard baseline operates on a context window of 256 tokens.
  • • Reducing context to 128 tokens cuts training time from 730s to 330s.
  • • Counterintuitively improves validation loss down to 1.49.
  • • Doubling context to 512 tokens slows training to over 1500s.
  • • Validation performance degrades severely to a loss of 2.01.
  • • Insight: Long context windows encourage overfitting and memorization at small scales.

Section 7

Capacity & Generalization

  • • Baseline model uses standard GELU activations.
  • • Highly expressive SwiGLU drops training loss to 0.53.
  • • However, validation loss worsens significantly to 1.88.
  • • Insight: Adding capacity in low-data regimes increases overfitting.
  • • Simpler ReLU activation improves validation loss to 1.60.
  • • Achieves superior generalization despite fitting the training data less closely.
  • • Insight: Simple functions with strong constraints generalize better at small scales.

Section 8

Positional Encoding

  • • Baseline utilizes standard Learned Position Embeddings.
  • • We swapped embeddings for advanced methods: RoPE and ALiBi.
  • • Both performed worse than baseline, rising to 1.74 (RoPE) and 1.81 (ALiBi).
  • • Removing Positional Encoding entirely achieved the best validation loss of 1.57.
  • • Insight: Causal masking and local datasets provide enough implicit position data.

Section 9

Transition to Key Result

  • • Most architectural modifications revealed clear tradeoffs between validation accuracy and speed.
  • • The most robust improvement came from a severe capacity constraint: Ternary Weight Quantization.

Section 10

Ternary Quantization

  • • Weights are strictly constrained to just three values: -1, 0, and 1.
  • • Implemented via a Straight-Through Estimator (STE) during the backward pass.
  • • Achieved the best validation loss in the entire study at 1.47.
  • • Extremely consistent across seeds, with an ultra-low variance of ±0.005.
  • • Insight: Severe weight restrictions act as a powerful form of implicit regularization.

Section 11

Efficiency Trade-off

  • • Plotting validation loss against runtime maps our design tradeoffs clearly.
  • • The standard Baseline rests in the center of a dense clusters of runs.
  • • The optimal Pareto Frontier is defined by just two models.
  • • Ternary Weight and Context-128 setups yield optimal efficiency.
  • • Insight: Simpler, constrained networks can be both faster and more accurate.

LESS IS MORE

Section 12

Conclusion

  • • Standard transformer design guidelines break down under strict data limits.
  • • Core architectural dependencies like Residual Connections remain completely mandatory.
  • • Expanding context windows or increasing activation complexity actively worsens overfitting.
  • • Strict constraints like Ternary Quantization act as effective regularization.
  • ⚡ Summary: Restricting capacity yields optimal generalization at small scales: less can often be more.
